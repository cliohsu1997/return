\begin{abstract}
With product returns, the textbook local welfare formula $dCS=-D(p)\,dp$
overstates the consumer burden of a price change: only kept units matter, so
$dCS=-D(p)(1-r(p))\,dp$.  Aggregates $(D,r)$ are sufficient for this
first-order measure.  Pass-through is unchanged in form; incidence uses kept
volume on the consumer side and producer surplus derived from profit
maximization.
\end{abstract}

%=============================================================================
\section{Setting}
%=============================================================================

Consumers pay list price $p$ at checkout, then learn a post-purchase match and
may return the item at hassle cost $\kappa_i$.  Producers are price-takers.
Each firm $j$ has production cost $C_j(q_j)$; returned units also cost $h$ to
handle.  Write $r(p)$ for the aggregate return rate and
$\mu(p)\equiv p-r(p)(p+h)$ for the effective margin per gross unit sold.

Equilibrium pairs list price $p$ with gross quantity $Q=D(p)$ such that
consumers optimize purchase and return decisions and producers optimize output
given effective margin $\mu(p)$.

%=============================================================================
\section{Consumer side}
%=============================================================================

Each consumer type $i$ has return hassle $\kappa_i$ and draws match quality
$u\sim G_i$ after purchase.  Expected surplus from buying at list price $p$ is
\[
  s_i(p)
  = \mathbb{E}_{u\sim G_i}\bigl[\max(u-p,\,-\kappa_i)\bigr].
\]
A type buys iff $s_i(p)\geq 0$; conditional on buying, it keeps the item iff
$u\geq p-\kappa_i$, with keep probability $k_i(p)=\Pr(u\geq p-\kappa_i)$.
Aggregating across types yields gross demand, kept volume, and the return rate:
\begin{align*}
  D(p)
  &= \int \mathbf{1}\{s_i(p)\geq 0\}\,dF(i),
  &\qquad
  K(p)
  &= \int \mathbf{1}\{s_i(p)\geq 0\}\,k_i(p)\,dF(i), \\
  r(p)
  &= 1 - K(p)/D(p).
\end{align*}

Total consumer surplus is $CS(p)=\int \mathbf{1}\{s_i\geq 0\}\,s_i\,dF$.
Differentiating along the price path, inframarginal buyers satisfy
$s_i'(p)=-k_i(p)$, while marginal buyers have $s_i=0$.  By the envelope
theorem,
\begin{equation}
  CS'(p) = -K(p) = -D(p)\bigl(1-r(p)\bigr),
  \label{eq:CS-prime}
\end{equation}
so a small price change affects welfare only through kept units:
\begin{equation}
  dCS = -D(p)\bigl(1-r(p)\bigr)\,dp,
  \qquad
  \Delta CS = -\int_{p_0}^{p_1} D(p)\bigl(1-r(p)\bigr)\,dp.
  \label{eq:dCS}
\end{equation}
The weight on $dp$ is kept volume $K$, not gross demand $D$.

%=============================================================================
\section{Supply side}
%=============================================================================

On the producer side, each firm $j$ takes effective margin $\mu$ as given and
chooses output to solve
\[
  \max_{q_j \geq 0}\;\; \mu\,q_j - C_j(q_j),
  \qquad
  \text{FOC: } C_j'(q_j)=\mu \;\;(\text{if } q_j>0),
\]
with $C_j(0)=0$.  Write $q_j(\mu)$ for firm $j$'s supply, set to zero when
$\mu$ is below its minimum marginal cost.

Summing across firms defines market supply and industry profit:
\begin{equation}
  S(\mu) \equiv \sum_j q_j(\mu),
  \qquad
  PS(\mu) \equiv \sum_j \bigl[\mu\,q_j(\mu)-C_j\bigl(q_j(\mu)\bigr)\bigr].
  \label{eq:agg-def}
\end{equation}
Which firms are active at each $\mu$ determines both the market quantity and
total profit.

Differentiating $PS(\mu)$ with respect to $\mu$ gives, for each firm,
\[
  \frac{d}{d\mu}\Bigl[\mu\,q_j-C_j(q_j)\Bigr]
  = q_j + q_j'(\mu)\,\bigl[\mu-C_j'(q_j)\bigr].
\]
Active firms satisfy $C_j'(q_j)=\mu$ at their chosen output, so the adjustment
term $q_j'(\mu)\,[\mu-C_j'(q_j)]$ is zero for each firm---equivalently, the
envelope theorem applied to each firm's profit problem gives
$d\pi_j/d\mu=q_j(\mu)$.  Summing over firms,
\[
  PS'(\mu)=\sum_j q_j(\mu)=S(\mu).
\]
With no production at $\mu=0$, it follows that industry profit equals the area
to the left of the market supply curve:
\begin{equation}
  PS(\mu)=\int_0^\mu S(x)\,dx.
  \label{eq:PS-integral}
\end{equation}
At equilibrium $\mu=\mu(p)$, $PS(\mu(p))=\int_0^{\mu(p)}S(x)\,dx$.

%=============================================================================
\section{Equilibrium}
%=============================================================================

Combining the two sides, equilibrium requires gross demand to equal aggregated
supply at effective margin $\mu(p)$:
\begin{equation}
  D(p) = S\bigl(\mu(p)\bigr),
  \qquad Q \equiv D(p) = \sum_j q_j\bigl(\mu(p)\bigr).
  \label{eq:clearing}
\end{equation}
Let $j^*$ denote a marginal active firm ($q_{j^*}>0$ and
$\mu=C_{j^*}'(q_{j^*})$).  Its per-unit production margin is zero in
equilibrium:
\begin{equation}
  m(p) \equiv \mu(p) - C_{j^*}'\!\bigl(q_{j^*}(\mu(p))\bigr) = 0.
  \label{eq:margin}
\end{equation}
Write $m_p\equiv dm/dp$ and $m_x\equiv \partial m/\partial x|_{Q,p}$.
Differentiating the clearing condition and the zero-margin condition along the
equilibrium path yields the pass-through objects used next.

%=============================================================================
\section{Pass-through}
%=============================================================================

Consider a small cost shock $x$, such as a uniform upward shift in every
$C_j'$ or an increase in handling cost $h$, and write $\rho_x\equiv dp/dx$.
Holding $(p,Q)$ fixed, a uniform production-cost shift lowers each firm's
margin by one dollar on the marginal unit, so $m_x=-1$; a handling shock gives
$m_x=-r(p)$.  Differentiating market clearing and the zero-margin condition
along the shocked equilibrium path then gives
\begin{equation}
  \rho_x
  = \frac{S'(\mu)\,m_x}{S'(\mu)\,m_p - D'(p)},
  \qquad
  \rho_h = r(p)\,\rho_C.
  \label{eq:pass-through}
\end{equation}
Here $S'(\mu)=\sum_j q_j'(\mu)$ is the aggregated slope of firm supply; no
single aggregate $C(Q)$ is needed.  In the homogeneous special case
($C_j=C$), $m_p=1-C''(Q)D'(p)-r'(p)(p+h)-r(p)$ and the formula above reduces
to the standard competitive pass-through when $r\equiv 0$.

%=============================================================================
\section{Incidence}
%=============================================================================

Combining the consumer first-order formula $dCS=-K\,dp$ with the supply-side
objects above, the welfare incidence of the shock is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dCS}{dx} = -K(p)\,\rho_x,
  \qquad
  \frac{dPS}{dx} = Q\bigl(m_p\,\rho_x + m_x\bigr).
  \label{eq:burden}
\end{equation}
The incidence ratio $I_x\equiv |dCS/dx|/|dPS/dx|$ is
\begin{equation}
  I_C = I_h
  = \frac{(1-r(p))\,S'(\mu)}{-D'(p)}.
  \label{eq:incidence}
\end{equation}
Production and handling shocks bear the same consumer share because
$\rho_h=r(p)\,\rho_C$ and the kept-volume factor $K=D(1-r)$ applies to both.
